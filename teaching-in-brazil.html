Quick Facts

Brazil at a glance

Brazil is a country of incredible energy — vibrant cities, stunning natural landscapes, world-famous festivals, and some of the warmest, most welcoming people you'll ever meet. For English teachers, it offers a low cost of living, genuine community integration, and strong demand from a rapidly growing middle class eager to learn English for business and travel.

São Paulo, Rio de Janeiro, and Brasília are the main hubs for English teaching, but opportunities exist nationwide in language schools, international schools, and private tutoring.

Cost of Living

What does life in Brazil actually cost?

Brazil offers an excellent quality of life at a relatively low cost — particularly for those earning in foreign currency or with savings. Local teacher salaries are modest but go far, especially outside São Paulo and Rio.

Living in Brazil

What to expect when you arrive

🏠
Finding Accommodation
Shared housing is common and affordable. Websites like OLX, QuintoAndar, and Zap Imóveis are widely used. Gated communities (condomínios) are popular for safety. Short-term furnished apartments via Airbnb are a practical option while settling in. Landlords typically require a local guarantor or 3 months' deposit.
🍳
Food & Culture
Brazilian food is bold, diverse, and deeply social. Churrasco (BBQ), feijoada, fresh tropical fruits, and açaí bowls are everyday staples. Eating out is very affordable — a restaurant lunch (prato feito) often costs R$20–R$35. Food markets are vibrant and a great way to experience local life.
🚍
Getting Around
São Paulo and Rio have metro systems. Buses are the primary transport in most cities. Ride-sharing apps (Uber, 99) are widely used and very affordable. Many teachers use a combination of public transport and apps. Traffic can be heavy in major cities — factor this into your commute planning.
🏥
Healthcare
Brazil has a public healthcare system (SUS) available to residents. Many expats and teachers opt for private health insurance (plano de saúde), which is affordable and gives access to excellent private hospitals and clinics. Your employer may include health insurance as part of your contract.
🎉
Culture & Social Life
Brazil's social culture is second to none. Brazilians are open, friendly, and love to socialise — football matches, beach days, samba nights, and Carnival are woven into everyday life. Learning Portuguese basics will unlock a completely different level of connection and community.
🔐
Safety Tips
Like any large country, Brazil requires sensible precautions — particularly in larger cities. Stay aware of your surroundings, avoid displaying expensive items, use trusted transport apps at night, and follow local advice. Expat communities are incredibly helpful in sharing up-to-date safety guidance for specific neighbourhoods.
Visas & Entry

How to get your work visa for Brazil

Brazil requires a work visa for foreign nationals taking up employment. The most common route for English teachers is the VITEM V (temporary work visa), sponsored by your employer. Brazil also offers digital nomad and investor visas that some teachers use creatively.

The process requires patience but is well-documented. Having a CELTA qualification gives you a strong advantage in securing sponsoring employers, particularly at reputable international language schools like CCAA, Cultura Inglesa, and Wizard.

1 Secure a job offer from a Brazilian employer
2 Employer applies to Brazilian Ministry of Labour
3 Apply at Brazilian consulate with work authorisation
4 Register with Federal Police within 90 days of arrival
Key Requirements
  • ✓ Bachelor's degree (any subject)
  • ✓ Signed job contract from Brazilian employer
  • ✓ Criminal background check (apostilled)
  • ✓ Valid passport (6+ months validity)
  • ✓ Work authorisation from Ministry of Labour
  • ✓ CELTA qualification (strongly preferred)
Processing time: 2–4 months. Start the process well before your intended travel date.
